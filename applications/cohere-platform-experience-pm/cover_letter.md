Cohere's position is unusual: you're the enterprise AI platform that doesn't force customers to trust a hyperscaler with their data. That's a real technical and product problem, not a marketing angle — and the PM who owns your API, SDK, and managed services layer needs to actually understand what's under the hood.

I'm a builder and a tinkerer. Seven years as an engineer at Google before I moved to PM, which means when your team debates API ergonomics or talks about how to surface model inference latency to a developer in a console, I'm not taking notes — I'm in the conversation. I've written eval harnesses in Python, prototyped RAG pipelines using LangChain and Anthropic's Claude, and built the kind of internal developer tooling that an org eventually depends on.

The specific work that feels most relevant here: at Smartsheet I built the evaluation infrastructure for the company's agentic AI platform from scratch. Three-tiered framework — human SME review, LLM-as-a-judge, and human-in-the-loop — covering 100+ scenarios and the statistical metrics needed to communicate pass/fail rates of non-deterministic systems to people who were used to unit tests. That's not a project that fit neatly on a roadmap. It required building something engineers could trust and then driving adoption across teams who hadn't asked for it.

That's the mode I work in: figure out what the platform needs to be reliable, then make the case for it.

On the developer product side — the console, the SDK ergonomics, the docs that make or break a first integration — I've been a deeply technical user of AI platforms and APIs for the last few years. I prototype before I prioritize. I've also been a researcher who published on input systems at ACM, so I think about developer experience the same way I think about any human-computer interface: the friction that doesn't feel like friction is the hardest problem.

I'd welcome a conversation about what the platform team is focused on building and where the gaps are.
