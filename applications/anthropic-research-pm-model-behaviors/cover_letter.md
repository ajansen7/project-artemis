I've been building AI evaluation infrastructure for the past two years — not because someone asked me to, but because I watched my company's agentic AI teams ship features with no principled way to know if they were actually working. I designed the 3-tiered eval framework, built the LLM-as-a-judge harness, drove cross-team adoption, and made the pragmatic call to ship phase one before the perfect system existed. Along the way I learned something that I think is directly relevant to the Research PM, Model Behaviors role: non-deterministic AI requires a fundamentally different definition of "done." The unit test mindset breaks. You define pass/fail logic and the system surfaces five correct answers when you expected three. The failure isn't the AI — it's the test.

That lesson came from a career arc that started in academic HCI research, ran through seven years of engineering at Google and Nest, and landed in AI product management. Before I was writing business cases, I was publishing at ACM CHI and UIST — building behavioral models of how people interact with systems, modeling error rates, designing input frameworks for users with motor impairments. The throughline across all of it has always been the same question: where does the system's model of human behavior break, and how do we make it fit people better instead of the other way around? That question feels very at home at Anthropic.

What I bring to this role specifically: I know what it takes to operationalize alignment thinking into shipping product. I've built taxonomies of model failure modes, defined behavioral metrics that hold up under live traffic (the golden-dataset rot problem is real), and made hard prioritization calls when the evaluation toolchain was evolving faster than the roadmap. I'm not a researcher who became curious about products — I'm a builder and a tinkerer who happens to think rigorously about systems and the humans inside them. I prototype to make arguments undeniable, I push for synthetic test injection before engineers think they need it, and I believe the most important artifact you can ship is sometimes a well-framed definition of "working."

The work Anthropic is doing on behavioral defaults and steerability isn't a research problem that eventually becomes a product problem — it's both at once, and it needs someone who can hold both without collapsing one into the other. That's the job I've been doing.
